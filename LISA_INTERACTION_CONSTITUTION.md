# THE LISA INTERACTION CONSTITUTION (LIC)

**Draft Version 1**

---

## PREAMBLE

We establish this Constitution as the supreme and enduring authority governing how human beings communicate with LISA.

LISA exists to serve persons who depend on deliberate, accessible, and dignified interaction. Every gesture, every navigation choice, every phrase, and every path through the system shall honor the user's intent, capacity, and safety.

This Constitution binds all present and future interaction within LISA. No feature, screen, flow, or capability may contradict its principles. Engineering charters, validation authorities, and implementations derive their authority from this document; they do not create interaction law independently.

The principles herein govern communication, navigation, accessibility, safety, and the orderly expansion of LISA for generations of use.

---

# CONSTITUTIONAL PRINCIPLES

The Articles in this Section establish the permanent philosophical foundations from which every Part, Title, Chapter, Section, and Article of this Constitution derives. Where any later provision admits ambiguity, these Principles shall govern its interpretation.

### PRINCIPLE 0 — ZERO-TOUCH (BRAIN 1 SUPREME RULE)

**Article 0.0.1.** LISA shall always assume that the primary user has no voluntary hand movement and cannot physically interact with the device after initial caregiver setup.

**Article 0.0.2.** Every Brain 1 workflow shall be completable using only eye movement, blink sequences, vision, and hearing. No Brain 1 feature may require touch after the caregiver has mounted the device, granted permissions, and pressed Start once.

**Article 0.0.3.** The conversation drives the interface. Lisa speaks; the interface changes; the user looks and blinks; Lisa responds. The interface shall never drive the conversation through tap-to-continue, swipe, or menu-driven progression for the primary user.

**Article 0.0.4.** Waiting is a feature. Lisa shall remain patient without arbitrary timeout pressure. Failed attempts shall never blame the user.

**Article 0.0.5.** Validation authority: `ZERO_TOUCH_PRINCIPLE_AUTHORITY_V1`. Implementation reference: `features/zero-touch-principle/README.md`.

### PRINCIPLE I — USER SOVEREIGNTY

**Article 0.1.1.** The user is always the supreme authority over communication. No system process, suggestion engine, caregiver action, or future capability shall supersede the user's authority to decide what is said, when it is said, and whether it is sent.

**Article 0.1.2.** LISA shall never speak, decide, or communicate on behalf of the user without deliberate user authority. Assistance may prepare, propose, or organize; it shall not usurp.

### PRINCIPLE II — COMMUNICATION BEFORE TECHNOLOGY

**Article 0.2.1.** Technology exists only to facilitate communication. Hardware, software, integration, and innovation are subordinate instruments, not ends in themselves.

**Article 0.2.2.** Engineering decisions shall never become more important than the user's ability to communicate. When technology and communication conflict, communication prevails.

### PRINCIPLE III — HUMAN DIGNITY

**Article 0.3.1.** Every interaction shall preserve the dignity, independence, and identity of the user. LISA shall not reduce the user to a passive recipient of system output or a subject of system control.

**Article 0.3.2.** The system exists to restore communication—not merely transmit words. Utterance shall reflect the user's personhood, agency, and voice.

### PRINCIPLE IV — INDEPENDENCE

**Article 0.4.1.** LISA shall maximize user independence while minimizing unnecessary dependence upon caregivers. Design shall prefer paths that enable the user to act without intermediary assistance wherever safety permits.

**Article 0.4.2.** Whenever two equally safe solutions exist, preference shall be given to the one requiring less caregiver intervention.

### PRINCIPLE V — LEAST EFFORT

**Article 0.5.1.** LISA shall always prefer the interaction requiring the least physical effort and the least cognitive effort while preserving communication accuracy and safety. Burden borne by the user is a cost to be minimized, not a tolerance to be assumed.

**Article 0.5.2.** The Principle of Least Effort shall govern navigation, gesture design, categories, vocabulary, scrolling, and all future interaction systems introduced within LISA.

**Article 0.5.3.** Least Effort shall not justify compromise of safety, user sovereignty, or clarity of meaning. Efficiency serves the user; it does not override constitutional protection.

### PRINCIPLE VI — LEARNABILITY

**Article 0.6.1.** LISA shall teach the user. The interaction system shall progressively guide users toward independent communication through visible explanation, consistent structure, and recoverable practice.

**Article 0.6.2.** The burden of learning shall primarily rest on the software rather than on the user or caregiver. Undocumented or memorized interaction law is a failure of design, not of user capacity.

### PRINCIPLE VII — ACCESSIBILITY FIRST

**Article 0.7.1.** Accessibility is not a feature. Accessibility is the governing purpose of every interaction within LISA.

**Article 0.7.2.** No capability shall be regarded as complete unless it is accessible through the modalities this Constitution requires. Accessibility precedes novelty, aesthetics, and expedience.

### PRINCIPLE VIII — SAFETY ABOVE CONVENIENCE

**Article 0.8.1.** Convenience may never reduce user safety. Shortcuts, automation, and simplification that introduce entrapment, ambiguity, or loss of user authority are prohibited regardless of efficiency gained.

### PRINCIPLE IX — CONSISTENCY

**Article 0.9.1.** If two situations appear identical to the user, they shall behave identically. Interaction consistency is a constitutional requirement, not a design preference.

**Article 0.9.2.** Divergent behavior under apparently identical conditions shall be remedied or made visibly distinct before the user is required to act.

### PRINCIPLE X — FUTURE COMPATIBILITY

**Article 0.10.1.** Future features shall extend the constitutional interaction language rather than replace it. Expansion adds to the user's stable vocabulary of movement and communication; it does not silently supersede it.

**Article 0.10.2.** Innovations incompatible with the interaction law established herein shall conform through amendment or redesign, not through informal parallel systems.

# FOUNDING PURPOSE

**Article 0.11.1.** LISA exists to restore communication to persons who cannot communicate through conventional speech. Every Article of this Constitution serves that end.

**Article 0.11.2.** This Constitution exists to preserve that mission regardless of future technology. No advance in hardware, software, artificial intelligence, or integration shall redefine LISA into a system that merely processes input without restoring dignified, user-directed communication.

**Article 0.11.3.** The Founding Purpose binds all Parts of this Constitution. Interpretation shall favor the restoration of communication, the sovereignty of the user, and the enduring dignity of persons whom LISA serves.

# PART I — NAVIGATION AUTHORITY

## TITLE I — GLOBAL NAVIGATION

### CHAPTER I — Supremacy of Global Commands

#### SECTION I — Definition and Scope

**Article 1.1.1.1.** Global navigation commands are the universal movement and control actions that apply throughout LISA regardless of the user's present location, mode, or activity.

**Article 1.1.1.2.** Global navigation commands shall remain consistent in meaning, availability, and gesture assignment across all modes and categories. No mode may redefine, conceal, or suspend a global command except as expressly permitted by this Constitution for emergency authority.

**Article 1.1.1.3.** Every global navigation action shall be clearly labelled. The user shall always be able to discern what a navigation action will do before committing to it.

#### SECTION II — Universal Reachability

**Article 1.1.1.4.** Categories shall always be reachable by the user from every mode and location within LISA.

**Article 1.1.1.5.** Back and Cancel shall always be reachable. The user shall never be required to complete an action, select a phrase, or remain in a state from which withdrawal is impossible.

**Article 1.1.1.6.** Emergency shall always be reachable. No screen, mode, overlay, or interaction state may obstruct, delay, or substitute for direct access to emergency authority.

### CHAPTER II — Labelling and Consistency

#### SECTION I — Clarity of Navigation

**Article 1.1.2.1.** Every navigation action presented to the user shall bear an explicit label describing its effect. Unlabelled navigation affordances are prohibited.

**Article 1.1.2.2.** Global navigation labels shall use stable, recognizable language. Synonyms that obscure meaning across modes are prohibited unless the underlying action is identical.

**Article 1.1.2.3.** When navigation actions are grouped or nested, each member of the group shall remain individually labelled. Collective or ambiguous group labels shall not replace individual clarity.

---

## TITLE II — NAVIGATION MODES

### CHAPTER I — Nature of Modes

#### SECTION I — Definition

**Article 1.2.1.1.** A navigation mode is a bounded interaction context within LISA that organizes vocabulary, pages, or specialized activity while remaining subject to global navigation authority.

**Article 1.2.1.2.** Modes exist to reduce complexity and orient the user. They shall not become enclosures from which the user cannot depart by ordinary global navigation.

**Article 1.2.1.3.** Each mode shall declare its identity to the user in plain language. The user shall always know which mode is active.

#### SECTION II — Mode Boundaries

**Article 1.2.1.4.** Entry into a mode shall be deliberate and reversible. Accidental entry into a mode shall be correctable without penalty or loss of user authority.

**Article 1.2.1.5.** Modes may alter local vocabulary and page content but shall not alter global navigation commands, their gestures, or their reachability.

**Article 1.2.1.6.** When a mode ends, LISA shall return the user to a known, labelled navigation state. Abandonment without recovery is prohibited.

### CHAPTER II — Mode Hierarchy

#### SECTION I — Subordination to Global Authority

**Article 1.2.2.1.** All modes are subordinate to global navigation. No mode may claim exclusive control over movement, cancellation, category access, or emergency.

**Article 1.2.2.2.** Conflicts between mode-specific behavior and global navigation shall be resolved in favor of global navigation and user recovery.

**Article 1.2.2.3.** Modes shall not stack in ways that obscure the user's present location or hide available recovery paths.

---

## TITLE III — CATEGORY HIERARCHY

### CHAPTER I — Organization of Categories

#### SECTION I — Purpose

**Article 1.3.1.1.** Categories organize communication into meaningful domains that reduce scrolling, searching, and cognitive load.

**Article 1.3.1.2.** The category hierarchy shall be comprehensible to the user. Depth shall serve clarity, not concealment.

**Article 1.3.1.3.** Every category shall be reachable through global navigation without dependence on memorized sequences or hidden shortcuts.

#### SECTION II — Hierarchy Rules

**Article 1.3.1.4.** Parent and child categories shall maintain a logical relationship apparent to the user. Orphaned or misleading hierarchy is prohibited.

**Article 1.3.1.5.** Category shortcuts may exist for fast access but shall not be the sole means of reaching a category required for basic communication.

**Article 1.3.1.6.** Leaving any category shall restore the user to a labelled navigation state from which further movement is possible.

### CHAPTER II — Category Integrity

#### SECTION I — Stability Across Modes

**Article 1.3.2.1.** Categories shall remain structurally consistent across modes. A category reachable in one mode shall not vanish without constitutional amendment or explicit user-facing notice of change.

**Article 1.3.2.2.** Vocabulary within a category may vary by page and context, but category identity and global access to it shall not.

**Article 1.3.2.3.** No category shall impersonate or duplicate emergency authority. Emergency is not a category.

---

## TITLE IV — USER MOVEMENT AUTHORITY

### CHAPTER I — The User as Navigator

#### SECTION I — Authority of Movement

**Article 1.4.1.1.** The user holds final authority over navigation within LISA. The system shall facilitate movement; it shall not compel it.

**Article 1.4.1.2.** LISA shall not advance the user to a new state without an intentional navigation act attributable to the user, except where recovery remains immediately available.

**Article 1.4.1.3.** The user must never become trapped. Every state shall offer at least one labelled path to Back, Cancel, Categories, or an equivalent recovery action defined by this Constitution.

#### SECTION II — Freedom of Departure

**Article 1.4.1.4.** Timers, prompts, and automated transitions shall not remove the user's ability to cancel or retreat during their active period.

**Article 1.4.1.5.** Forced progression through sequences without skip or exit constitutes entrapment and is prohibited.

**Article 1.4.1.6.** When the user invokes Back or Cancel, LISA shall honor that intent promptly and predictably.

### CHAPTER II — Predictability of Movement

#### SECTION I — Consistent Consequences

**Article 1.4.2.1.** Identical navigation actions shall produce identical results under identical conditions across LISA.

**Article 1.4.2.2.** Navigation history shall not be used to surprise the user with irreversible outcomes. Destructive or consequential actions require deliberate confirmation.

**Article 1.4.2.3.** The user's present page, category, and mode shall be reflected in what LISA processes and presents, so that movement and communication remain aligned with user expectation.

# PART II — GESTURE LANGUAGE

## TITLE I — GLOBAL GESTURE AUTHORITY

### CHAPTER I — Reserved Global Gestures

#### SECTION I — Supremacy

**Article 2.1.1.1.** Global gestures are reserved for navigation and system authority. They shall not carry vocabulary meaning and shall not be reassigned to local communication purposes.

**Article 2.1.1.2.** Reserved global gestures may not be repurposed. Once assigned constitutional meaning, a global gesture retains that meaning for the life of the system unless amended by explicit constitutional process.

**Article 2.1.1.3.** Global gestures shall remain active and unambiguous in every mode. No mode may suspend a global gesture without violating this Constitution.

#### SECTION II — Constitutional Global Assignments

**Article 2.1.1.4.** The following global gesture assignments are established as constitutional law:

| Gesture | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| L2 R0 | Scroll Up / Previous |
| L0 R2 | Scroll Down / Next |
| L1 R1 | Select / Save |
| L2 R2 | Back / Cancel |
| L4 R4 | Categories |
| L6 R0 | Emergency |

**Article 2.1.1.5.** Each global gesture shall be explained to the user in visible, labelled form wherever gesture input is accepted.

**Article 2.1.1.6.** Global gestures shall take precedence over local gestures when both could apply. Ambiguity at the global layer is a safety failure.

### CHAPTER II — Gesture Economy

#### SECTION I — Efficiency and Reuse

**Article 2.1.2.1.** Short, reusable gestures are preferred over long or sequential gestures for actions the user performs frequently.

**Article 2.1.2.2.** Gesture sequences that differ only subtly shall not be used for unrelated actions within the same mode.

**Article 2.1.2.3.** Gesture design shall minimize fatigue. Repetition required for ordinary navigation shall remain within human endurance for the intended user population.

---

## TITLE II — LOCAL VOCABULARY GESTURES

### CHAPTER I — Local Scope

#### SECTION I — Definition

**Article 2.2.1.1.** Vocabulary gestures are local. They apply within a category, page, or mode context and shall not override global gestures.

**Article 2.2.1.2.** The same local gesture may mean different things in different categories or pages, provided that no two meanings are active simultaneously in the same mode and on the same page.

**Article 2.2.1.3.** Local gesture meaning shall be determined by the active category and page. LISA shall process vocabulary gestures for the current page only, unless the user has explicitly navigated to a broader context.

#### SECTION II — Contextual Clarity

**Article 2.2.1.4.** When a local gesture is available, its present meaning shall be visible or immediately discoverable on the current page.

**Article 2.2.1.5.** Local gestures shall not silently change meaning while the user remains on the same page without a visible indication of change.

**Article 2.2.1.6.** Vocabulary gestures shall support first-person communication. Phrase and word selection shall enable the user to speak as themselves, not as the system.

### CHAPTER II — Local Gesture Discipline

#### SECTION I — Uniqueness Within Context

**Article 2.2.2.1.** No gesture may have two active meanings in the same mode on the same page. One gesture, one meaning, one context.

**Article 2.2.2.2.** Overloading a local gesture with conditional branches that the user cannot discern constitutes ambiguous gesture assignment and is prohibited.

**Article 2.2.2.3.** Local gestures that resemble global gestures shall be avoided where confusion is likely.

---

## TITLE III — RESERVED GESTURES

### CHAPTER I — Reservation Policy

#### SECTION I — Protection of the Vocabulary

**Article 2.3.1.1.** Gestures assigned to global navigation, emergency, or other constitutional functions are reserved and shall not appear as vocabulary selections on any page.

**Article 2.3.1.2.** New features shall not consume reserved gestures for convenience. Expansion must find alternative local assignments or await constitutional amendment.

**Article 2.3.1.3.** Experimental or provisional gesture assignments shall not violate reservation. Experimentation belongs outside production interaction paths or within explicitly labelled non-production contexts not governed as user-facing LISA.

### CHAPTER II — Gesture Namespace

#### SECTION I — Separation of Concerns

**Article 2.3.2.1.** Global and local gesture namespaces shall remain logically separate. A gesture's classification as global or local shall be knowable and stable.

**Article 2.3.2.2.** Future hardware shall map physical input to the same logical gesture identifiers before any meaning is applied, preserving reservation across devices.

**Article 2.3.2.3.** Third-party integrations shall not register gestures that collide with reserved global assignments.

---

## TITLE IV — GESTURE SAFETY

### CHAPTER I — Accidental Activation

#### SECTION I — Single-Gesture Protection

**Article 2.4.1.1.** A single accidental gesture must not trigger communication. Speaking, saving, or sending meaningful output requires deliberate action beyond isolated unintentional input.

**Article 2.4.1.2.** Where sensitivity to brief input is necessary, LISA shall favor confirmation or compositional intent for consequential communication acts.

**Article 2.4.1.3.** Debounce, dwell, confirmation, or equivalent deliberate-commit mechanisms shall protect against spurious vocabulary activation without blocking intentional use.

### CHAPTER II — Ambiguity as Failure

#### SECTION I — Safety Standard

**Article 2.4.2.1.** Ambiguous gestures are safety failures. A gesture that could reasonably be interpreted as two different actions in the same context violates this Constitution.

**Article 2.4.2.2.** Gesture fatigue that leads to elevated error rates shall be treated as a safety concern and remedied through design revision, not user adaptation alone.

**Article 2.4.2.3.** When gesture conflict is discovered, global and emergency assignments shall be preserved first; local assignments shall yield.

# PART III — COMMUNICATION HIERARCHY

## TITLE I — CATEGORY ORGANIZATION

### CHAPTER I — Communication Domains

#### SECTION I — Purpose of Categories

**Article 3.1.1.1.** Communication is organized by categories so that related phrases and vocabulary remain together and accessible without exhaustive scrolling.

**Article 3.1.1.2.** Categories shall reflect human communication needs, not internal system structure. Organization serves the user, not the implementer.

**Article 3.1.1.3.** Category shortcuts may be provided for fast access, but shortcuts shall supplement—not replace—the reachable category hierarchy.

#### SECTION II — Reduction of Burden

**Article 3.1.1.4.** Categories exist to reduce scrolling and fatigue. Layout and grouping shall prefer fewer, clearer motions over dense undifferentiated lists.

**Article 3.1.1.5.** Related categories may be nested, but nesting shall not hide essential daily communication behind excessive depth.

**Article 3.1.1.6.** Each category shall present a coherent scope so the user can predict what vocabulary lies within before entering.

---

## TITLE II — VOCABULARY PAGES

### CHAPTER I — Page Structure

#### SECTION I — Context-Specific Pages

**Article 3.2.1.1.** Phrase pages are context-specific. Vocabulary on a page shall belong to the active category and shall support the communication situations that category represents.

**Article 3.2.1.2.** Pages shall be navigable with global scroll and selection gestures without breaking category or mode context.

**Article 3.2.1.3.** Page titles or headings shall identify context so the user always knows which vocabulary set is active.

#### SECTION II — Page Boundaries

**Article 3.2.1.4.** Vocabulary from one page shall not silently act as if it belongs to another. Cross-page effects require explicit navigation.

**Article 3.2.1.5.** Empty, placeholder, or incomplete pages shall not masquerade as complete vocabulary sets in production communication paths.

**Article 3.2.1.6.** Page transitions shall preserve user orientation. The user shall know when the page has changed and what changed.

---

## TITLE III — PHRASE DESIGN

### CHAPTER I — Meaningful Utterance

#### SECTION I — Phrase over Fragment

**Article 3.3.1.1.** Full meaningful phrases are preferred over isolated words where a phrase better expresses human intent and dignity.

**Article 3.3.1.2.** Isolated words remain permissible where brevity is essential, but they shall compose into first-person, user-attributable communication.

**Article 3.3.1.3.** Phrases shall be grammatically and socially complete enough to stand alone when spoken or displayed to a listener.

#### SECTION II — First-Person Voice

**Article 3.3.1.4.** Vocabulary shall support first-person communication. The user speaks; LISA facilitates. System-voice impersonation of the user is prohibited.

**Article 3.3.1.5.** Phrase design shall respect the user's agency in what is said, when it is said, and whether it is sent.

**Article 3.3.1.6.** Stock phrases shall remain editable or dismissible where personalization is necessary for accurate self-expression.

#### SECTION III — Natural Communication and Listener Clarity

**Article 3.3.1.7.** Communication shall sound natural when spoken or displayed. Phrase design shall favor utterances that a listener would recognize as ordinary human speech rather than mechanical or system-generated fragments.

**Article 3.3.1.8.** First-person communication is preferred. Vocabulary and phrasing shall enable the user to express needs, feelings, and identity as themselves, in their own voice.

**Article 3.3.1.9.** Complete phrases are preferred over isolated words whenever practical. Where a complete phrase conveys intent more clearly and with less listener ambiguity, it shall be favored over single-word selections.

**Article 3.3.1.10.** Communication shall reduce ambiguity for caregivers and listeners. Output shall be sufficiently clear in context that those who receive it can understand the user's intent without guesswork or specialized knowledge of the system.

---

## TITLE IV — CONTEXTUAL COMMUNICATION

### CHAPTER I — Context Determines Meaning

#### SECTION I — Active Context

**Article 3.4.1.1.** Phrase meaning depends on the active category and page. The same surface selection may differ in implication by context, and that contextual dependence shall be visible or inferable to the user.

**Article 3.4.1.2.** Current-page-only processing reduces mistakes. LISA shall interpret vocabulary in light of the page and category the user is on, not distant or inactive contexts.

**Article 3.4.1.3.** Context switches shall be user-initiated or clearly signalled. Silent context change during communication is prohibited.

#### SECTION II — Output Discipline

**Article 3.4.1.4.** Communication output shall reflect the user's explicit selection on the active page unless the user has confirmed a composed message drawn from multiple deliberate acts.

**Article 3.4.1.5.** Predictive or suggested completions may assist selection but shall not speak, send, or finalize communication without user confirmation.

**Article 3.4.1.6.** Listeners and caregivers shall be able to trust that displayed or spoken output corresponds to the user's deliberate choices within the active context.

# PART IV — ACCESSIBILITY

## TITLE I — EYE-ONLY OPERATION

### CHAPTER I — Primary Modality

#### SECTION I — Complete Eye Access

**Article 4.1.1.1.** LISA must support eye-only operation. Every navigation, communication, and emergency action necessary for independent use shall be achievable through eye-based input alone.

**Article 4.1.1.2.** Eye-only operation shall not be a degraded subset of full functionality. It is a first-class requirement, not an alternate mode of lesser standing.

**Article 4.1.1.3.** Every eye action must be visibly explained. The user shall see what each available eye gesture or dwell target will do before commitment.

#### SECTION II — Eye Input Clarity

**Article 4.1.1.4.** Visual feedback for eye selection, dwell progress, and confirmation shall be perceptible without reliance on color alone.

**Article 4.1.1.5.** Eye input settings, including sensitivity and response time, shall be user-controllable within safe bounds.

**Article 4.1.1.6.** Eye-only paths to Back, Cancel, Categories, and Emergency shall remain unobstructed in all production interaction states.

---

## TITLE II — HUMAN TOUCH PARITY

### CHAPTER I — Touch as Equal Path

#### SECTION I — Requirement

**Article 4.2.1.1.** LISA must also support human touch parity for caregivers, helpers, testers, and the founder. Touch is a legitimate input modality, not a secondary hack.

**Article 4.2.1.2.** Every visible action must be tappable if touch input exists on the device. Nothing visible shall be eye-only when touch is available unless constitutionally required for safety confirmation distinct from navigation.

**Article 4.2.1.3.** Touch and eye input shall invoke the same logical actions. Parity means equivalence of outcome, not duplication of difficulty.

#### SECTION II — Assistance Without Usurpation

**Article 4.2.1.4.** Caregiver or helper touch shall not override user authority without explicit, visible mode for assisted operation.

**Article 4.2.1.5.** Touch parity supports testing, setup, and aid. It shall not silently bypass confirmation or safety rules that bind eye input.

**Article 4.2.1.6.** When both touch and eye input are active, feedback shall indicate which input path is governing a given action when distinction matters for safety.

---

## TITLE III — VISUAL CLARITY

### CHAPTER I — Labelling and Legibility

#### SECTION I — Nothing Unlabelled

**Article 4.3.1.1.** Icons and arrows must not appear without labels. Symbol alone shall not carry navigation or communication meaning.

**Article 4.3.1.2.** Text must remain readable. Type size, contrast, and spacing shall support sustained use by persons with low vision, fatigue, or cognitive load.

**Article 4.3.1.3.** Labels shall use plain language aligned with global navigation terminology established by this Constitution.

#### SECTION II — Honest Presentation

**Article 4.3.1.4.** Decorative elements shall not mimic actionable controls. If it looks actionable, it must be actionable and labelled.

**Article 4.3.1.5.** Disabled or unavailable actions shall be visibly distinct from available actions. False affordances are prohibited.

**Article 4.3.1.6.** Status information essential to orientation—mode, category, page, and pending confirmation—shall remain visually accessible during interaction.

### CHAPTER II — Self-Teaching Interaction

#### SECTION I — Explanation Without Memorization

**Article 4.3.2.1.** Every visible action shall explain itself. The user shall not be required to infer the effect of a control from symbol, position, or prior experience alone.

**Article 4.3.2.2.** Users shall never have to memorize invisible interaction rules. Rules that govern behavior shall be discoverable on the surface of interaction or through progressive in-context guidance.

**Article 4.3.2.3.** The interface shall continuously teach the interaction language. Global gestures, navigation paths, and vocabulary conventions shall be reinforced through visible labels, consistent placement, and contextual explanation throughout use.

#### SECTION II — Guided Independence

**Article 4.3.2.4.** Teaching shall serve independence, not dependence. Guidance shall diminish as the user demonstrates mastery of stable interaction patterns, without withholding recovery or safety information.

**Article 4.3.2.5.** Caregivers and helpers shall not be the primary repository of interaction knowledge that LISA could present directly to the user.

---

## TITLE IV — USER FATIGUE REDUCTION

### CHAPTER I — Endurance by Design

#### SECTION I — Interaction Load

**Article 4.4.1.1.** Interaction must reduce fatigue. Repetitive motion, excessive depth, and unnecessary confirmation shall be minimized consistent with safety.

**Article 4.4.1.2.** Settings that affect physical or cognitive load—including sensitivity, dwell time, scroll step, and feedback delay—shall be user-controllable.

**Article 4.4.1.3.** Fatigue reduction shall not remove safety confirmations required for consequential or irreversible acts.

#### SECTION II — Sustainable Communication

**Article 4.4.1.4.** Category and page design shall prefer frequent needs along shorter paths than rare needs along long ones.

**Article 4.4.1.5.** Rest breaks, cancellation, and escape from intensive input sequences shall remain available without penalty.

**Article 4.4.1.6.** LISA shall treat sustained error or abort rates as signals that interaction design—not user capability—is failing.

---

# PART V — SAFETY & EMERGENCY

## TITLE I — EMERGENCY AUTHORITY

### CHAPTER I — Nature of Emergency

#### SECTION I — Not Vocabulary

**Article 5.1.1.1.** Emergency is not vocabulary. It shall not be listed among phrases, categories, or communicative pages as though it were a thing to say in conversation.

**Article 5.1.1.2.** Emergency is a system authority. It invokes protected behavior distinct from ordinary communication and navigation.

**Article 5.1.1.3.** Emergency shall override all modes. When invoked, mode-specific rules, local gestures, and non-critical prompts yield to emergency handling.

#### SECTION II — Visibility and Reach

**Article 5.1.1.4.** Emergency must be visible and reachable everywhere within LISA's production interaction surface.

**Article 5.1.1.5.** Emergency shall be assigned to the constitutional gesture L6 R0 and shall retain that assignment unless amended by explicit constitutional process.

**Article 5.1.1.6.** Nothing shall interpose confirmation layers that materially delay emergency invocation beyond what is strictly necessary to distinguish emergency from accidental adjacent input, and such distinction shall favor rapid access.

---

## TITLE II — USER RECOVERY

### CHAPTER I — Escape from Every State

#### SECTION I — Recovery Routes

**Article 5.2.1.1.** Recovery routes must exist from every mode. The user shall always have a labelled path to a less restrictive, safer state.

**Article 5.2.1.2.** Users must never be trapped. Entrapment in any form—modal, gestural, temporal, or rhetorical—is a constitutional violation.

**Article 5.2.1.3.** After error, timeout, or partial completion, LISA shall offer clear recovery without blame-oriented messaging that obstructs escape.

#### SECTION II — Return to Known Ground

**Article 5.2.1.4.** Recovery shall prefer return to Categories or an equivalent globally labelled home of navigation when local context is lost.

**Article 5.2.1.5.** Emergency recovery shall not depend on completing non-emergency steps first.

**Article 5.2.1.6.** If the user invokes Back or Cancel repeatedly, LISA shall interpret that as a demand for retreat and shall not escalate commitment requirements.

### CHAPTER II — Dead Ends and Proportional Recovery

#### SECTION I — Prohibition of Dead Ends

**Article 5.2.2.1.** Navigation dead ends are constitutional violations. No interaction state shall present the user with only forward, irreversible, or non-labelled paths when retreat is required or reasonably expected.

**Article 5.2.2.2.** A user becoming trapped in any interaction state is a constitutional failure. Entrapment is not mitigated by caregiver rescue, documentation, or user training.

**Article 5.2.2.3.** Recovery shall always be simpler than the action that caused the error. Correcting a mistaken navigation act, accidental entry, or partial completion shall require no more effort—and wherever possible, less effort—than the act that produced the error state.

#### SECTION II — Recovery as First-Class Design

**Article 5.2.2.4.** Recovery paths shall be labelled, reachable, and predictable. The user shall not need specialized knowledge to escape error or uncertainty.

**Article 5.2.2.5.** When multiple recovery options exist, LISA shall prefer the option that returns the user to the nearest stable, labelled navigation state with the least effort consistent with safety.

---

## TITLE III — FAILURE PREVENTION

### CHAPTER I — Classes of Safety Failure

#### SECTION I — Navigation and Gesture

**Article 5.3.1.1.** Navigation conflicts are safety failures. Two navigation actions with overlapping outcomes in the same context shall not coexist.

**Article 5.3.1.2.** Ambiguous gestures are safety failures. Uncertainty about whether an act navigates, speaks, or triggers emergency is prohibited in production paths.

**Article 5.3.1.3.** Hidden state—where the user cannot determine location, available gestures, or consequences—is a safety failure.

#### SECTION II — Communication Safety

**Article 5.3.1.4.** Unconfirmed predictive communication must not speak or send. Assistance that puts words in the user's mouth without confirmation is prohibited.

**Article 5.3.1.5.** Destructive or high-consequence actions shall require deliberate confirmation distinct from ordinary Select or Save.

**Article 5.3.1.6.** Safety failures shall be remedied before release. They are not acceptable defects to be documented away.

---

## TITLE IV — NAVIGATION SAFETY

### CHAPTER I — Safety Through Navigation Design

#### SECTION I — Structural Safeguards

**Article 5.4.1.1.** Global navigation consistency is a safety mechanism. Inconsistency across modes increases error and shall be treated as defect, not preference.

**Article 5.4.1.2.** Category, Back, Cancel, and Emergency reachability shall be verified as safety invariants, not mere usability goals.

**Article 5.4.1.3.** Overlays, dialogs, and temporary states shall preserve access to recovery and emergency unless constitutionally superseded by active emergency handling itself.

#### SECTION II — Conflict Resolution

**Article 5.4.1.4.** When safety and convenience conflict, safety prevails.

**Article 5.4.1.5.** When local vocabulary and global navigation conflict, global navigation and recovery prevail.

**Article 5.4.1.6.** No feature may bypass constitutional safety rules for expedience, aesthetics, or commercial pressure.

---

# PART VI — FUTURE EXPANSION

## TITLE I — NEW CATEGORIES

### CHAPTER I — Expansion Within Law

#### SECTION I — Category Addition

**Article 6.1.1.1.** New categories must obey the same navigation system as existing categories. They shall be reachable, labelled, and escapable.

**Article 6.1.1.2.** New categories shall not introduce parallel navigation schemes that bypass global commands.

**Article 6.1.1.3.** Removal or reorganization of categories shall preserve user access to essential communication or provide explicit migration paths.

---

## TITLE II — NEW INTERACTION MODES

### CHAPTER I — Mode Expansion

#### SECTION I — Subordination

**Article 6.2.1.1.** New modes must obey global navigation. Mode novelty shall not suspend Back, Cancel, Categories, or Emergency.

**Article 6.2.1.2.** Modes introduced for specialized integrations shall identify themselves clearly and shall exit cleanly to the general navigation model.

**Article 6.2.1.3.** Modes shall not multiply hidden layers. Depth is permitted; obscurity is not.

---

## TITLE III — FUTURE HARDWARE

### CHAPTER I — Device Independence

#### SECTION I — Translation to Constitutional Commands

**Article 6.3.1.1.** Future hardware must translate physical input into the same navigation commands and gesture identifiers defined by this Constitution before meaning is applied.

**Article 6.3.1.2.** New sensors or controllers shall not redefine global gestures at the device layer.

**Article 6.3.1.3.** Hardware failure shall degrade gracefully, preserving recovery and emergency where physically possible.

---

## TITLE IV — FUTURE AI CAPABILITIES

### CHAPTER I — AI Under User Authority

#### SECTION I — Non-Usurpation

**Article 6.4.1.1.** Future AI features must not override user authority. Prediction, ranking, and suggestion serve the user; they do not replace the user.

**Article 6.4.1.2.** Predictive communication must not speak without user confirmation. AI-generated utterances shall not be transmitted as though user-selected.

**Article 6.4.1.3.** AI that alters navigation, suppresses global commands, or hides recovery paths is prohibited.

#### SECTION II — Assistive Boundaries

**Article 6.4.1.4.** AI may reduce search burden within a category or page but shall not silently change active context.

**Article 6.4.1.5.** AI confidence or automation shall be visible when it influences what the user sees or selects.

**Article 6.4.1.6.** The user shall retain the power to reject, edit, or ignore AI suggestions without penalty to navigation or access.

---

## TITLE V — CONSTITUTIONAL COMPATIBILITY

### CHAPTER I — Integrations

#### SECTION I — Fit Within the Model

**Article 6.5.1.1.** Video calling, smart home control, wheelchair interfaces, and future integrations must fit into the constitutional interaction model. They shall use global navigation, respect emergency supremacy, and remain escapable.

**Article 6.5.1.2.** Integrations shall not introduce vocabulary that masquerades as emergency or global navigation.

**Article 6.5.1.3.** Third-party surfaces embedded within LISA remain subject to this Constitution while the user is within LISA's care.

#### SECTION II — Long-Term Coherence

**Article 6.5.1.4.** Features intended for temporary experiment shall not erode permanent constitutional guarantees in production paths.

**Article 6.5.1.5.** Expansion that cannot comply shall wait for amendment or redesign, not informal exception.

**Article 6.5.1.6.** This Constitution shall remain sufficient in itself to govern interaction law without dependence on transient implementation knowledge or undocumented convention.

---

# PART VII — CONSTITUTIONAL ENFORCEMENT

## TITLE I — ENGINEERING CHARTERS

### CHAPTER I — Derivation of Authority

#### SECTION I — Subordinate Documents

**Article 7.1.1.1.** Engineering charters derive from this Constitution. They translate constitutional principles into buildable requirements but shall not contradict them.

**Article 7.1.1.2.** Where a charter is silent, this Constitution governs. Where a charter conflicts with this Constitution, the charter is void to the extent of conflict.

**Article 7.1.1.3.** No team, vendor, or contributor may claim authority to invent interaction law outside the constitutional framework.

---

## TITLE II — VALIDATION AUTHORITIES

### CHAPTER I — Validators and Proof

#### SECTION I — Source of Validation Rules

**Article 7.2.1.1.** Validators derive from engineering charters and this Constitution. They exist to detect violation, not to define new interaction policy.

**Article 7.2.1.2.** Validators must enforce navigation safety, including reachability of Back, Cancel, Categories, and Emergency, and freedom from entrapment.

**Article 7.2.1.3.** Validators shall treat ambiguous gestures and navigation conflicts as hard failures unless explicitly exempted by constitutional amendment.

#### SECTION II — Scope of Validation

**Article 7.2.1.4.** Validation shall cover all production interaction paths, including integrations and mode-specific surfaces.

**Article 7.2.1.5.** Absence of a validator does not excuse absence of compliance. The Constitution binds behavior whether or not automated proof yet exists.

**Article 7.2.1.6.** Validators shall be updated when this Constitution is amended, before amended rules take effect in production.

---

## TITLE III — COMPLIANCE REQUIREMENTS

### CHAPTER I — Invalid Implementations

#### SECTION I — Consequences of Violation

**Article 7.3.1.1.** Implementations that violate constitutional interaction rules are invalid. They shall not ship as user-facing LISA.

**Article 7.3.1.2.** Partial compliance is insufficient where safety invariants are at stake. Emergency reachability and user recovery are non-negotiable.

**Article 7.3.1.3.** Waivers, flags, or developer overrides that bypass constitutional interaction rules in production are prohibited.

#### SECTION II — Continuous Obligation

**Article 7.3.1.4.** Compliance is ongoing. Refactors, optimizations, and new features re-open the obligation to conform.

**Article 7.3.1.5.** Regression of constitutional guarantees shall be treated as defect with priority equal to or greater than functional breakage.

**Article 7.3.1.6.** Documentation of non-compliance is not a substitute for remediation.

---

## TITLE IV — AMENDMENT PROCESS

### CHAPTER I — Changing the Law

#### SECTION I — Explicit Amendment

**Article 7.4.1.1.** Amendments must be explicit. Changes to interaction law require documented amendment to this Constitution or a duly adopted successor instrument proclaimed as such.

**Article 7.4.1.2.** No feature may bypass the Constitution for convenience. Expediency is not amendment.

**Article 7.4.1.3.** Amendments shall state what prior Article they modify or supersede and shall preserve safety invariants unless the amendment consciously and visibly accepts a defined exception.

#### SECTION II — Order of Effect

**Article 7.4.1.4.** Upon amendment, engineering charters and validators shall be revised to reflect the new law before the amended rule governs production.

**Article 7.4.1.5.** Users shall be informed of amendments that materially change gestures, navigation, or emergency behavior.

**Article 7.4.1.6.** Amendments shall not relocate enduring interaction law into subordinate documents without explicit constitutional authority. What is withdrawn from this Constitution ceases to bind unless re-adopted by formal amendment.

---

## DRAFTING NOTE — GUIDANCE FOR ENGINEERING CHARTERS

*The following guidance informs charter authors and drafters. It does not itself constitute constitutional law.*

When evaluating whether a proposed rule belongs in this Constitution versus an engineering charter, charter authors shall apply the **enduring-law criterion**: if LISA were rebuilt from first principles in a distant future, would this rule still govern human interaction with the system? Principles that would remain true belong in this Constitution; rules tied to transient technology, implementation method, or release mechanics belong in charters derived hereunder.

The spirit of that criterion is already embodied in the Constitutional Principles and Founding Purpose. Charters shall implement; they shall not silently redefine.
